Current Interpretation

What looks real

  • Engram is not flat at small scale: BF5 already reaches the same broad regime as the built-in bigram runs.
  • Per-head values plus canonicalized tokens gave a small, repeatable-looking win.
  • BF99 with row-count LR scaling, ENGRAM_LR_FLOOR=0.5, layers 2+6, layer-specific hashes, and per-layer readouts is the best completed Engram result so far, improving BF39 dense floor from 3.2858 to 3.2770.
  • BF78 and BF99 did not improve final loss just by adding rows; row scaling needed the larger LR schedule to show a final gain.
  • For BF99, linear row-count LR scaling is hot but viable with the floor: update/param stays around 6e-2 to 7e-2 through step 500, then cools to about 1.2e-2 by final.
  • Adding a second shared-table insertion at layer 6 gives a small final gain, matching the direction of the official Engram two-layer placement.
  • Layer 6 alone is not enough: it starts faster at step 250 but loses the advantage by step 500/750.
  • Layer 5 as the second insertion point is bad at step 250, so the 2+6 placement is specific rather than a generic two-layer effect.
  • Layer-specific hashes for layers 2+6 are a completed win: final 3.2774 versus 3.2828 for shared hashes, with essentially the same table parameter count.
  • Separate per-layer readouts add a smaller completed win on top: final 3.2770 versus 3.2774.
  • Equal-total layer table partitions are negative: step-500 loss 7.2727 versus 7.2152 for the shared-table readout run.
  • The BF5-80 scaling suite is cleanly monotonic at final loss, with BF80 reaching 3.2816 and BF99 still ahead at 3.2770.
  • BF99 with 96-dim rows fit at 5.74B table params, but did not improve step-500 loss, so row width alone is not the missing scaling knob.
  • CPU/offloaded optimizer paths are workable, but throughput cost is large and can confound comparisons.

Open mystery

  • More rows reduce collisions and still improve final loss through BF80/BF99, but BF198 with partitions/vector Adam did not beat the shared-bank BF99 run.
  • The floor sweep brackets the useful BF39 value: 0.25 and 1.0 were both worse than 0.5 at step 1000; BF99 confirms that the LR scale/floor interaction can matter more than raw table size alone.
  • Dense Adam moments are too expensive at larger scale; SparseAdam keeps BF99 resident, but the BF39 replay shows a small late optimizer-path gap.
  • A bounded sparse Adam tail did not recover dense behavior: tail-1 was worse at step 500 and tail-4 was too memory-heavy.
  • We now log both touched-row and zero-including table RMS for sparse Engram updates, so future row-scaling runs can be compared without mixing reporting conventions.

Next Queue

  1. Use the BF5-99 curve to fit a simple scale trend and decide whether BF120/BF160 is worth trying with resident SparseAdam or a memory-saving optimizer.
  2. Only test fully separate per-layer tables if we can afford more total table capacity; equal-total partitions were negative.
  3. Compare against the official Engram recipe again: tokenizer compression, layer placement, hash mix, and any scaling/regularization details now matter more than raw table size.
  4. Try a smaller readout bottleneck change that is closer to Engram's own implementation rather than naive untied projections or MLA-style shared up-projection.
  5. Consider BF99 dim-768 full reruns only for variance estimates; LR 8.5 and dim 1152 were both negative at step 500.
  6. Revisit vectorial Adam only if memory pressure blocks a specific official-recipe comparison; previous row-scalar second moment was negative at BF99.
  7. Quantization path: FSQ/BSQ or row-wise int8 for cold Engram weights is attractive after architecture behavior is understood; do not combine with LR mystery yet.
  8. Revisit ROM only if Engram readout saturates: ROM can be faster per row at smaller state, but write/read dynamics have already shown stability issues.
